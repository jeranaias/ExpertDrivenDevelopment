What EDD Is and Isn't

Expert-Driven Development is a structured methodology for solving real operational problems using AI-assisted tools on the Microsoft Power Platform. It is not a technology experiment or a side project. Here is the distinction:

EDD IS EDD IS NOT
A structured process with defined steps, approvals, and quality checks An uncontrolled free-for-all where anyone builds anything
Building tools that solve real, documented problems in your section Building tools for fun or to learn technology for its own sake
Marines describing problems in plain language; AI handles implementation Marines learning to code or becoming IT professionals
Using approved platforms (SharePoint, Power Apps, Power Automate, Power BI) Using unauthorized software, personal accounts, or shadow IT
Time-boxed efforts with supervisor approval and defined outcomes Open-ended projects with no deadline or accountability
Documented solutions with SOPs so anyone can maintain them One person's secret tool that breaks when they PCS

Instructor Note

This comparison addresses the most common supervisor concern: "Is this just people playing on computers?" Make it clear that EDD has structure, accountability, and measurable outcomes. If supervisors do not buy in, their Marines will not get the time or support they need to succeed.

Time Allocation

Building tools takes time, but it saves more time than it costs. Here is what to expect:

Phase First Tool Subsequent Tools
Training 2 – 6 hours (Orientation + Platform Training) 0 hours (already trained)
Problem Definition 1 – 2 hours (with supervisor) 30 – 60 minutes
Building 4 – 8 hours (learning while building) 2 – 4 hours (experienced)
Testing & Feedback 1 – 2 hours 1 hour
Documentation 1 hour 30 minutes
Total 9 – 19 hours 4 – 6 hours

Return on Investment

A tool that saves 30 minutes per week across a 30-person platoon saves 780 person-hours per year. Most first tools pay for themselves within two weeks. Subsequent tools are built faster and pay off sooner.

Evaluating Problem Definitions

Before a Marine starts building, they must submit a problem definition for your approval. Here is what to look for and what to flag.

What to Verify

  • The problem is real: Is this an actual pain point in the section, or a solution looking for a problem?
  • The scope is small enough: Can this be built in under 8 hours? If not, can it be broken into smaller pieces?
  • The users are identified: Who will use this tool? Have they been consulted about what they need?
  • The data is appropriate: Does the tool handle PII, classified information, or sensitive data? If so, it requires additional review.
  • The outcome is measurable: How will you know if the tool is successful? What metric improves?

Red Flags

Stop and Discuss If You See These

  • "I want to build something cool." — No defined problem. Redirect to identifying an actual pain point.
  • "This will replace the entire [major system]." — Scope too large. Break it down or defer to IT.
  • "Only I understand how it works." — No documentation plan. Require documentation before approval.
  • "It needs to connect to [external system]." — May require IT involvement for connectors or credentials.
  • "I'll work on it in my free time." — EDD should be allocated duty time, not personal time. If it is worth building, it is worth scheduling.

Your Role in the Workflow

As a supervisor, you have five specific responsibilities in the EDD workflow:

  1. Approve problem definitions. Review the submitted problem definition. Verify it meets the criteria above. Either approve it with a time allocation or send it back with specific feedback on what needs to change.
  2. Allocate protected build time. Schedule specific blocks of time for your Marine to build. This is not "when you get a chance" time. Put it on the training schedule. Protect it from tasking conflicts. A Marine who gets interrupted every 15 minutes will never finish a tool.
  3. Check in at milestones. Do not wait until the tool is "done" to review it. Check in at three points: after the data structure is built, after the first working screen or flow, and before final deployment. Early feedback prevents wasted effort.
  4. Ensure documentation exists. Before a tool goes into production use, verify that documentation exists: what the tool does, who uses it, how to maintain it, and who to contact if it breaks. A tool without documentation is a tool that will become a problem.
  5. Report outcomes. Track and report the impact of EDD tools in your section. How much time was saved? What process improved? This data justifies continued investment in the program and helps other sections identify similar opportunities.

Instructor Note

The most common failure mode for EDD is not technical — it is supervisors who approve projects but never allocate time, or who approve time but never check in. Emphasize that active supervision makes the difference between a tool that ships and a tool that dies on someone's desktop.

Where to Find Help

When you or your Marines need assistance, here is where to go:

  • EDD SOP: Standard Operating Procedure — the full process from problem definition through deployment and maintenance.
  • Prompt Toolkit: Interactive Toolkit — pre-built prompt templates for common scenarios. Marines should start here before writing prompts from scratch.
  • Training Courses: Course Catalog — Orientation, Platform Training, and Advanced Workshop curricula with all examples and exercises.
  • Resources: Resource Library — links to Microsoft documentation, community forums, and reference materials.
  • Direct Support: Contact the EDD program coordinator for questions about process, policy, or technical issues that cannot be resolved through self-service resources.

Key Takeaway

Your role is not to understand the technology. Your role is to understand the problem, protect the time, check the progress, and verify the documentation. The process works when supervisors are engaged. It stalls when they are not.
